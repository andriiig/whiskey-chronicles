By the letter of federal regulation, Jack Daniel's is bourbon. It meets
every technical requirement: a mash bill that's corn-majority, distilled
to no more than 160 proof, entered into new charred oak barrels at no
more than 125 proof, aged in the United States with no added coloring
or flavoring. Nothing in federal law would stop the company from
printing "bourbon" on the label. It has chosen not to, consistently,
since well before Tennessee ever made the distinction a matter of state
law. The company's own materials have long insisted, in effect, that
Jack Daniel's simply isn't bourbon, on principle, regardless of what the
federal definition would technically permit.

## One extra step before the barrel

The thing standing between Jack Daniel's and a bourbon label is a step
that happens before the spirit ever touches wood: the Lincoln County
Process. Before barreling, the freshly distilled spirit is dripped
slowly — sometimes taking several days to fully pass through — down
through a deep bed of packed sugar-maple charcoal, typically several
feet thick, filtering through the charcoal by gravity rather than being
pumped or forced. The charcoal itself is made specifically for this
purpose: distilleries burn stacks of sugar maple boards on-site,
producing charcoal that's then ground and packed into tall vats for
the whiskey to filter through.

The mechanism is mellowing more than removal. The charcoal adsorbs some
of the harsher, more volatile congeners in the raw distillate — the
compounds most responsible for the sharp, hot, slightly grainy edge that
young, unfiltered whiskey carries straight off the still — before the
spirit ever begins its years of interaction with charred oak. The result
is a smoother starting point going into the barrel, which producers
argue changes the entire character of the finished whiskey enough to
justify treating it as a separate category rather than a stylistic
variant of bourbon.

## Why "Lincoln County" doesn't mean much geographically anymore

The name is a historical artifact more than a current geographic fact.
The process is named for Lincoln County, Tennessee, where Jack Daniel's
distillery was originally located when the county lines were drawn
differently in the 19th century. Tennessee's county boundaries were
later redrawn, and the distillery's actual location — Lynchburg — ended
up in what's now Moore County. The name never got updated to match; it
stuck to the process itself, independent of which county the map
currently assigns the distillery to.

## From marketing choice to statute

For most of the 20th century, the distinction between Tennessee whiskey
and bourbon was entirely a matter of company preference, not law — Jack
Daniel's called itself Tennessee whiskey because it wanted to, and
nothing would have stopped a competitor from skipping the charcoal step
while still calling their product Tennessee whiskey, or from using the
process while calling their product bourbon.

That changed in 2013, when Tennessee passed a state law formally
defining "Tennessee Whiskey" as a protected term: to use the label, a
spirit must be made in Tennessee, meet the same core compositional
standards as bourbon, and be filtered through maple charcoal before
barreling — codifying the Lincoln County Process as a legal requirement
rather than a voluntary house style. The law included one notable
carve-out: a grandfather clause exempting a specific existing Tennessee
distillery (Benjamin Prichard's) that had been making whiskey without
the charcoal-filtering step and didn't want to be forced to change its
existing product to keep using the state name.

So the category that started as one company's marketing preference —
essentially a stylistic choice to differentiate from bourbon in the
eyes of consumers — is now enforced by state statute, with a legally
protected name, a mandatory production step, and a specific carved-out
exception for the one incumbent producer that predated the rule and
didn't fit it. Very little of that legislative structure existed when
Jack Daniel's first decided, generations earlier, that it simply wasn't
going to call itself bourbon.

## A distinction that's mostly about identity, not chemistry

Blind tasting panels and whiskey writers have long debated how much the
Lincoln County Process actually changes the finished flavor compared to
an equivalent bourbon aged the same way without the charcoal step — the
honest answer, based on most published side-by-side evaluations, is
"noticeably, but not dramatically." The bigger effect may be
commercial and cultural rather than purely sensory: Tennessee whiskey
gets to exist as its own recognized category with its own shelf space,
its own state-level legal identity, and its own marketing story, instead
of being one more bourbon competing for attention inside a much larger
and more crowded category dominated by Kentucky. Sometimes the most
consequential ingredient in a spirit's identity isn't in the mash bill
or the barrel at all — it's the decision, backed eventually by state
law, to insist on a different name.

## The scale that makes the exception interesting

What makes Jack Daniel's specifically worth examining, rather than
Tennessee whiskey as a category in the abstract, is sheer volume: the
Lynchburg distillery is one of the best-selling whiskey brands on earth,
consistently ranking among the top few whiskey brands worldwide by
cases sold, in a category — American whiskey broadly — dominated by
bourbon-labeled competitors many times its number. A production process
invented as one distillery's point of differentiation has scaled to
become the single most recognizable "not bourbon" in the world, while
sitting a short drive from Kentucky distilleries making a chemically
similar product under a different name and a much smaller average
market share per brand.

That scale is also why the 2013 law reads less like disinterested
regulation and more like the codification of one company's existing
market position. Tennessee whiskey as a legally defined category exists
in a form that fits Jack Daniel's practice almost exactly, with a single
grandfathered exception carved out for the one incumbent that didn't
already do things that way. Laws defining spirits categories are rarely
written in a vacuum; they tend to formalize whatever the dominant
existing producer was already doing, and Tennessee's 2013 statute is a
particularly clean example of that pattern in action.
