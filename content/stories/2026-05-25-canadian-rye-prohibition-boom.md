United States Prohibition, from 1920 to 1933, was catastrophic for
American distillers — legally shut out of their own domestic market for
thirteen years, many closed permanently, their equipment scrapped or
sold, their brands and market position gone by the time repeal finally
arrived. On paper, Prohibition should have been close to irrelevant to
Canadian distillers: alcohol remained perfectly legal to produce in
Canada throughout the entire period, and Canadian law placed no
restriction on manufacturing whisky for export. In practice, that narrow
legal gap between "illegal to import into the US" and "legal to produce
and export from Canada" turned the Canada–US border — especially the
short stretch of the Detroit River separating Windsor, Ontario from
Detroit, Michigan — into the busiest smuggling corridor in North
America.

## Legal at the source, illegal at the destination

Hiram Walker's distillery in Windsor and Seagram's operations further
inland expanded aggressively through the 1920s, building distilling and
warehousing capacity far beyond anything Canada's own comparatively
small domestic market could plausibly absorb on its own. Canadian
production wasn't being scaled to serve Canadians. It was being scaled
to serve a market of over a hundred million Americans who legally
couldn't buy alcohol at home but very much still wanted to.

The distillers themselves generally weren't the ones piloting boats
across the Detroit River at night — that direct legal exposure belonged
to rum-runners and the organized crime networks who took physical
possession of the liquor and handled the actual smuggling into US
territory, absorbing the greatest legal risk in the chain. What Hiram
Walker and Seagram did, entirely within Canadian law, was sell enormous
volumes of finished whisky into bonded export warehouses sitting a
short boat ride from some of Prohibition's thirstiest American cities —
and Canadian export law at the time didn't require them to verify or
police where "exported" liquor ended up after it left Canadian
jurisdiction. The distillers stayed on the legal side of a line that
existed specifically because the liquor's next stop, just across the
water, was illegal.

## A head start nobody else got to keep

When Prohibition ended with US repeal in 1933, the two industries came
out of the same thirteen years in dramatically different shape. Most
American distillers had to rebuild essentially from nothing — lost
equipment, lost brands, lost distribution relationships, and, critically,
no aged whiskey in the warehouse, because there had been no legal
domestic production for over a decade. Whisky doesn't age itself in
thirteen years of downtime; every barrel that would have been maturing
through the 1920s simply didn't exist.

Canadian producers walked out of the same period with mature,
well-aged inventory sitting in warehouses, established and
internationally recognized brand names, and functioning
distribution relationships built up over more than a decade of
supplying (legally, from Canada's side of the border) the largest
liquor market in the world by other means. Seagram in particular used
the position it built during Prohibition to expand into the American
market directly and aggressively once repeal made that legal, becoming
one of the dominant spirits companies in North America for the middle
of the 20th century. Crown Royal itself has a more specific origin
tied to this same era of Canadian distilling ambition: Seagram created
it in 1939 to mark the royal visit of King George VI and Queen
Elizabeth to Canada, drawing on the same distilling infrastructure and
market position the company had spent the Prohibition years building.

## Why the head start mattered longer than a decade

That imbalance — mature stock and established brands on one side,
a rebuild-from-scratch on the other — is a substantial part of why
Canadian whisky held a dominant share of the US whisky market well into
the postwar decades, a position that would have been far harder to
establish if both countries' industries had entered the 1930s on equal
footing. American bourbon and rye producers spent years just getting
back to pre-Prohibition production levels and rebuilding aged
inventory, while Canadian brands were already sitting on shelves with a
decade-plus of continuous operation behind them.

It's an unusual case in industry history where a country's producers
benefited enormously from a law they were never subject to, purely by
sitting on the correct side of a border from a market that banned the
thing they made. Prohibition is conventionally told as an American
story about enforcement, speakeasies, and organized crime. From north
of the border, it reads as a supply-side growth story — one where the
distillers who profited most were simply the ones close enough to sell
into a prohibition without ever being inside one.

## The rye style that came along for the ride

Canadian whisky's reputation for a lighter, smoother, rye-forward style
also has roots in this same period of forced international selling.
Producers competing for American drinkers who were used to sourcing
liquor from a patchwork of illicit domestic sources — often rough,
inconsistent, occasionally dangerous moonshine — found that a
lighter-bodied, more consistently blended product traveled well, was
easy to drink without much dilution, and stood out from whatever
rougher alternatives a speakeasy might otherwise be pouring. That
consumer preference, formed under smuggling conditions in the 1920s,
shaped house styles that Canadian producers kept refining and exporting
long after 1933 made the whole workaround unnecessary.

It's worth noting the label "rye whisky" in Canada doesn't require the
same rye-grain-majority mash bill that US law demands of anything
called rye whiskey — Canadian regulations use "Canadian whisky" and "rye
whisky" close to interchangeably, regardless of the actual grain
composition, a looser legal standard that itself traces back to a
national industry that spent its formative export decades selling into
a market it wasn't technically allowed to access, with correspondingly
less domestic regulatory pressure to standardize grain-specific naming
the way the US eventually did.
