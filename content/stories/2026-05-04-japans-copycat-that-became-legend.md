Masataka Taketsuru sailed to Scotland in 1918 as a young chemistry
graduate sent by his employer, Settsu Shuzo, to learn Western liquor
production firsthand. He enrolled at the University of Glasgow, studied
organic chemistry, and — more importantly for whisky history —
apprenticed at several working distilleries, including Longmorn and
Bo'ness, taking detailed handwritten notes on mash bills, still shapes,
peat levels, and cask management. Those notebooks later became something
close to the founding technical blueprint for the entire Japanese whisky
industry. Taketsuru wasn't trying to invent a new style of whisky. He
was trying to reproduce Scotch as faithfully as Japanese ingredients,
climate, and equipment would allow — an explicit, unapologetic act of
imitation, undertaken as a formal research assignment.

He came home in 1920 with a Scottish wife, Jessie Roberta "Rita" Cowan,
and a set of technical knowledge that Japan's fledgling liquor industry
had never had access to before. Settsu Shuzo, facing financial trouble,
shelved its whisky ambitions before Taketsuru could put much of that
knowledge to use — so in 1923, he was hired instead by Shinjiro Torii,
founder of what would become Suntory, to help build Japan's first
serious malt whisky distillery at Yamazaki, just outside Kyoto.

## Choosing a site by resemblance, not convenience

The Yamazaki site wasn't picked for ease of transport or cost. Torii and
Taketsuru chose it specifically because its climate and water — the
convergence of three rivers, persistent fog, humid summers — reminded
them of Scotland's distilling regions. That's a telling decision: the
entire founding premise of the operation was fidelity to a foreign
template, not adaptation to what Japan's climate might do differently or
better. Stills, techniques, and even some early equipment leaned on
Scottish precedent as closely as geography and 1920s shipping would
allow.

Taketsuru and Torii's partnership didn't last. After building Yamazaki
and helping establish what would become Suntory's whisky business,
Taketsuru left in 1934 to found his own company, further north on
Hokkaido — a location he chose this time specifically because its
colder climate and terrain resembled Scotland even more closely than
Yamazaki did. That company became Nikka. Japan's two flagship whisky
houses — Suntory and Nikka — both trace their founding logic back to the
same instinct: find the place in Japan that looks and feels most like
Scotland, and build there.

## Decades of being taken seriously as a copy, nothing more

For most of the 20th century, that's precisely how the outside world
treated Japanese whisky, on the rare occasions it noticed it at all: a
technically competent, respectfully executed copy of Scotch, worth
acknowledging for craft but not worth taking seriously as its own
tradition. Almost none of it was exported in meaningful volume before
the 1980s. Suntory and Nikka spent decades refining blends and malts
largely for a domestic Japanese market, competing with each other more
than with anyone abroad, essentially invisible to the international
whisky press and to Scotch's own home market.

Domestically, the category went through its own volatile cycles —
booming through the 1970s and into the '80s as whisky became a symbol of
postwar prosperity and Western sophistication, then collapsing through
the 1990s as Japanese drinking habits shifted toward shochu and other
spirits, to the point that several distilleries scaled back production
or mothballed stock rather than keep distilling into a shrinking market.
Whisky that would turn out, twenty years later, to be extraordinarily
well-aged and valuable was in some cases sitting unsold or underpriced
during this slump, simply because nobody especially wanted it at the
time.

## The blind tasting that changed the argument

The reversal wasn't a marketing campaign. It was a series of blind
competition results that international judges couldn't reconcile with
their assumptions. Yamazaki 12-year won "Best of the Best" at the 2003
International Spirits Challenge. Then, more dramatically, Nikka's
Yoichi 10-year won "Best of the Best" at the World Whisky Awards in
2008 and Suntory's Hibiki 21-year took the World's Best Blended Whisky
title the same year — awards being handed to Japanese expressions in
head-to-head blind tastings against the Scotch producers the entire
category had spent 80 years openly imitating.

Within roughly a decade of that first Yamazaki win, demand outpaced
supply badly enough that both Suntory and Nikka discontinued several
core age-stated expressions entirely — including, eventually, Yamazaki
12 itself for a period, and equivalents at Nikka — because two decades
of underinvestment during the domestic slump of the 1990s meant there
simply wasn't enough well-aged stock in the warehouse to meet a global
demand nobody had planned for. The category's biggest problem went, in
under fifteen years, from "nobody outside Japan wants this" to
"there isn't enough of this left to sell."

## What actually changed, and what didn't

Japanese whisky isn't a copy that eventually became original through
some creative departure from the Scottish template — the mash bills,
pot still shapes, and maturation philosophy still owe an enormous,
openly acknowledged debt to what Taketsuru brought back in his
notebooks in 1920. What changed was recognition, not method: judges
running blind tastings had no brand-driven expectation to protect,
and the whisky itself, on its own terms, was frequently better than
what it had spent ninety years being politely compared against. Japan
didn't out-innovate Scotland. It out-executed the thing it had set out,
from the very beginning, to imitate — and it took the rest of the world
nearly a century to taste that difference honestly.
